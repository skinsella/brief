# Findings for policy

A reading of the 100 NSB occupational groups, the 2019–2024 LFS growth rates,
and the Friend-or-Foe two-axis exposure scores in [the visualiser](https://stephenkinsella.net/irishjobs/).

- AI exposure is irreducibly two-dimensional: the *complementary* axis (AI augments the worker) and the *substitutable* axis (AI displaces the work) move employment in opposite directions, so any single-number "AI risk" metric used in policy will mislead by averaging two opposite mechanisms.
- High-complementary occupations grew +6.8% in 2019–2024 vs +1.0% for low-complementary, meaning the policy frame should be augmentation-first not displacement-first.
- High-substitutable occupations grew +2.2% vs +3.9% for low-substitutable — a 1.7pp slowdown, not a decline — so the displacement is showing up at the hiring margin where active labour market policies actually work.
- The LLM exposure scores in the visualiser correlate at **Pearson r = +0.75** with the academic Felten/Raj/Seamans (2021) AIOE benchmark across 99 of 100 occupations, with the complementary axis correlating most strongly (r = +0.74) — the methodology is anchored, not idiosyncratic.
- Where the LLM and AIOE disagree, the LLM correctly downweights physical and care work (childminders, kitchen assistants, care workers — LLM 1/10 vs AIOE 5–6/10), reflecting GPT-4-class reality better than 2021's pure ability-matching framework, which is a meaningful methodological improvement worth noting.
- The Department of Finance's −32% drop in under-30 ICT employment over 2023Q1–2025Q4 is the most consequential single Irish AI labour-market finding to date and deserves dedicated monitoring with each new LFS release.
- Public CSO statistics do not break out age × occupation, so the entry-level squeeze is only visible via microdata (RMF) access — making it imperative that SOLAS, EGFSN, and the Department of Finance have privileged access to that microdata for early-warning monitoring.
- The squeeze is concentrated on mid-pay clerical, sales, and basic professional occupations (€30–50K), so the political and distributional pressure will land on the middle of the income distribution — exactly the cohort that pays the bulk of marginal income tax and has the highest political salience.
- About 62% of Irish employment sits in high-or-medium DoF risk sectors compared to ~45% for the OECD average, meaning Ireland's exposure is a national-economic risk concentrated by an exceptional reliance on ICT, financial services, and professional services.
- Ireland is simultaneously hosting the data centres and AI hardware imports (€1.9B in Q4 2025 alone) that power the disruption, putting it in the unusual position of capturing infrastructure investment income while exporting its own clerical labour market.
- Reskilling and FET budgets should be targeted at the specific substitutable occupations the data identifies — customer-service supervisors, payroll clerks, basic accountants, paralegals, sales administrators — not the older "automation-at-risk" categories from the pre-LLM literature.
- The pay distribution is widening at both ends — augmented knowledge workers above, physical-care workers below, with the middle squeezed — so income-tax progressivity and the universal-services welfare model become more important as redistribution mechanisms.
- Ireland's national productivity statistics are confounded by multinational tax structures, but the firm-level productivity dividend in high-complementary sectors should be a near-term measurement priority for the CSO and Department of Finance.
- Ireland's FET system, EGFSN's skills work, and SOLAS's reskilling brief are well-placed in principle to address an entry-level squeeze; whether they are positioned for the specific occupations the substitutable axis flags is an empirical question worth a targeted review.
- The exposure scores are calibrated to roughly GPT-4-class AI capability, so a step-change in model capability (likely in the next 2–3 generations) could open the current 1.7pp slowdown into an absolute decline — the visualiser will detect this in the 2027 LFS release if it happens.
- The right policy posture at current AI capability is monitoring at quarterly resolution and targeted reskilling at the entry-level margin, not emergency intervention — the labour market is being reshaped, not in crisis.

Fiscal Incidence by Income Decile

What households receive in cash social transfers versus what they pay in income tax and social insurance, by position in the income distribution. Weekly amounts from CSO SILC 2024.

Data source: All decile figures are from CSO SILC 2024, Table 2.3a — "Average weekly nominal household income by net disposable household income deciles." Income reference period: calendar year 2023. Deciles are ranked by net disposable household income. Downloaded from cso.ie.
What this does NOT capture: The "benefits" side shows only cash social transfers (pensions, jobseeker's, child payments, etc.). It excludes the value of public services received — health, education, social housing, and subsidised childcare — which disproportionately benefit lower-income households. It also excludes indirect taxes (VAT, excise, carbon tax) which are regressive and fall more heavily on lower deciles as a share of income. The true net fiscal transfer to the bottom deciles is substantially larger than shown. See Callan & Keane (2009, ESRI) for Irish estimates of non-cash benefit incidence — though now 17 years old, this remains the most recent study.

SILC coverage: The survey covers private households only. People in direct provision, nursing homes, hospitals, prisons, and those who are homeless are excluded — these populations, among the most dependent on state services, would sit below Decile 1 if captured. Means within deciles (especially D10) are pulled by outliers; medians would tell a different story.

The Gini coefficient measures inequality from 0% (perfect equality) to 100% (one household has everything). Ireland's tax and welfare system reduces the Gini by 18.1 percentage points.

Where You Sit in the Distribution

Based on CSO SILC 2024: median household disposable income is €58,922/year (€1,133/week).

Caveat: SILC decile thresholds are based on total household disposable income (all earners combined). Comparing a single earner's take-home pay against household thresholds will understate where a multi-earner household actually sits.

Temporal mismatch: The decile thresholds reflect 2023 incomes (SILC 2024). The tax calculator uses 2026 rates. With ~5%/year wage growth since 2023, today's thresholds would be higher — so this comparison may place you in a higher decile than you'd actually occupy in 2026.
